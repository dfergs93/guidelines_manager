# ACR Contrast Safety — Iodinated & Gadolinium Contrast Agents

??? info "Reference Information"
    **Full Name:** ACR Manual on Contrast Media
    **Version:** 2023 (v11)
    **Publisher:** American College of Radiology (ACR) Committee on Drugs and Contrast Media
    **Application:** All contrast-enhanced CT, MRI, and fluoroscopic studies
    **Last Institutional Review:** January 2024
    **Modifications:** None

!!! danger "Emergency Reference"
    For acute contrast reactions: see [Acute Reaction Treatment](#acute-contrast-reaction-treatment) below.

---

## Iodinated Contrast Media (ICM)

### Adverse Reaction Classification

=== "Acute Reactions (within 1 hour)"

    | Severity | Symptoms | Treatment |
    |----------|----------|-----------|
    | **Mild** | Limited urticaria, nausea/vomiting, pruritus, pallor/diaphoresis | Observation, diphenhydramine if hives are bothersome |
    | **Moderate** | Diffuse urticaria, mild bronchospasm, facial edema, tachycardia/bradycardia | Diphenhydramine, consider epinephrine if bronchospasm |
    | **Severe** | Diffuse bronchospasm, laryngeal edema, anaphylactic shock, cardiac arrest | Epinephrine (IM), oxygen, IV fluids, call emergency services |

=== "Delayed Reactions (1–72 hours)"

    - Predominantly skin reactions (maculopapular rash, urticaria)
    - Usually self-limited; treat with oral antihistamines or steroids if needed
    - Incidence: ~2% after ionic, ~1% after non-ionic contrast

=== "Late Reactions (>72 hours)"

    - Thyroid dysfunction (contrast-induced hyperthyroidism) — especially with high iodine load
    - Metformin-related lactic acidosis risk

## Acute Contrast Reaction Treatment

!!! danger "Acute Reaction Management Protocol"

=== "Urticaria (Mild)"

    1. Observe patient
    2. If symptomatic: **Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV/IM**
    3. If extensive: consider escalation

=== "Bronchospasm"

    1. Oxygen via face mask (6–10 L/min)
    2. **Albuterol (beta-2 agonist) inhaler** — 2–3 puffs; may repeat
    3. If no improvement or severe: **Epinephrine 0.3 mg IM** (1:1,000) in anterolateral thigh
    4. Call emergency services if severe

=== "Laryngeal Edema"

    1. Oxygen via face mask
    2. **Epinephrine 0.3 mg IM** (1:1,000) — may repeat every 5–15 min
    3. Call emergency services immediately
    4. Prepare for possible intubation

=== "Anaphylaxis / Anaphylactic Shock"

    1. Call emergency services (Code Blue)
    2. **Epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg IM** (1:1,000) — anterolateral thigh; may repeat every 5–15 min
    3. Supine position (legs elevated if tolerated)
    4. Oxygen via face mask (10–15 L/min)
    5. IV access; **normal saline 1–2 L bolus** for hypotension
    6. Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV (adjunct only, NOT primary treatment)
    7. If refractory: epinephrine infusion, vasopressors

=== "Vasovagal Reaction"

    1. Elevate legs (Trendelenburg position)
    2. Oxygen if needed
    3. IV fluids for hypotension
    4. **Atropine 0.6–1 mg IV** if persistent bradycardia

## Premedication for High-Risk Patients

### Indications for Premedication

!!! info "Who Needs Premedication?"

    - **Prior moderate or severe** acute allergic-like reaction to same class of contrast agent
    - **Multiple** prior mild reactions to same class
    - History of asthma (increased risk of severe reactions)

    !!! note "No premedication proven to eliminate risk entirely"

### Standard Premedication Regimens

=== "Elective (13-hour)"

    | Drug | Dose | Timing |
    |------|------|--------|
    | Methylprednisolone | 32 mg PO | 13 hours before |
    | Methylprednisolone | 32 mg PO | 1 hour before |
    | Diphenhydramine (optional) | 50 mg PO/IM/IV | 1 hour before |

=== "Alternative (12-hour)"

    | Drug | Dose | Timing |
    |------|------|--------|
    | Prednisone | 50 mg PO | 13 hours before |
    | Prednisone | 50 mg PO | 7 hours before |
    | Prednisone | 50 mg PO | 1 hour before |
    | Diphenhydramine (optional) | 50 mg PO | 1 hour before |

=== "Accelerated (3–5 hour)"

    | Drug | Dose | Timing |
    |------|------|--------|
    | Methylprednisolone | 40 mg IV | Every 4 hours until contrast |
    | Diphenhydramine | 50 mg IV | 1 hour before |

    !!! warning "Accelerated regimens are less effective — use only when 12-hour regimen is not feasible"

## Contrast and Renal Function (ICM)

### Contrast-Induced Acute Kidney Injury (CI-AKI)

!!! info "Risk Stratification for CI-AKI"
    True CI-AKI is less common than historically believed. Risk is primarily significant in patients with severely reduced GFR.

    | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²) | Risk Level | Recommendation |
    |----------------------|------------|---------------|
    | ≥45 | Low | Contrast may be given; no additional precautions |
    | 30–44 | Moderate | IV hydration if feasible; consider benefit vs. risk |
    | <30 (not on dialysis) | Elevated | Use with caution; discuss with ordering physician; IV hydration |
    | Dialysis-dependent | N/A | Contrast may be given; coordinate dialysis timing if possible |

=== "IV Hydration Protocol"

    For patients at risk (eGFR 30–44 or risk factors):

    - **Normal saline 100 mL/hr** IV — 6 hours before and 6 hours after contrast, OR
    - **Sodium bicarbonate** 1.26% solution 3 mL/kg/hr × 1 hour before, then 1 mL/kg/hr × 6 hours after (alternative)

=== "N-Acetylcysteine"

    **Not recommended** — evidence does not support use for CI-AKI prevention.

## Metformin Guidelines

!!! warning "Metformin and Iodinated Contrast"

    Risk of lactic acidosis is low but can be serious. Current ACR guidance:

    | eGFR | Action |
    |------|--------|
    | ≥30 (intra-arterial or intravenous) | **No need to hold metformin** — low risk in hemodynamically stable patients with eGFR ≥30 |
    | <30 OR acute kidney injury expected | Hold metformin at time of contrast; do not restart for 48 hours; recheck renal function before resuming |
    | Intra-arterial contrast with eGFR 30–60 | Consider holding metformin; reassess renal function at 48 hours |

## Gadolinium-Based Contrast Agents (GBCA)

### Agent Risk Classification

!!! danger "NSF Risk Grouping"
    Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis (NSF) is a rare but serious fibrosing disorder in patients with severely reduced renal function.

    | Group | Agents | NSF Risk |
    |-------|--------|----------|
    | **Group I (Highest)** | Gadodiamide (Omniscan), gadoversetamide (OptiMARK), gadopentetate dimeglumine (Magnevist) | High — avoid in eGFR <30 or AKI |
    | **Group II (Moderate)** | Gadobenate dimeglumine (MultiHance), gadofosveset (Ablavar) | Intermediate — caution in eGFR <30 |
    | **Group III (Lowest)** | Gadobutrol (Gadavist), gadoteridol (ProHance), gadoterate meglumine (Dotarem), gadoterate macrocyclic agents | Negligible — preferred in at-risk patients |

    !!! tip "Preferred Agents"
        Use macrocyclic ionic or non-ionic agents (Group III) when GBCA is required in patients with eGFR <30.

### GBCA and Renal Function

| eGFR | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| ≥30 | Standard doses of any GBCA are acceptable; Group III preferred |
| 15–29 | Group III preferred; use lowest effective dose; document risk discussion |
| <15 or dialysis-dependent | Risk vs. benefit discussion required; Group III agents; avoid Group I/II |
| AKI | Avoid GBCA if possible; if necessary, Group III only |

## GBCA and Special Populations

=== "Pregnancy"

    !!! warning "GBCA in Pregnancy"
        - Gadolinium crosses the placenta; association with adverse effects found in some epidemiologic studies
        - **Use only when clearly necessary and benefit outweighs risk**
        - Document informed consent discussion
        - Prefer lowest effective dose; avoid repeated exposure
        - MRI without contrast is preferred first for pregnant patients

=== "Lactation"

    !!! info "GBCA During Breastfeeding"
        - <0.04% of GBCA dose is excreted into breast milk; <1% of that is absorbed by infant
        - **ACR guidance:** GBCA administration is safe to continue breastfeeding; interruption is not necessary
        - However, if patient requests: may pump-and-discard for 24 hours for peace of mind

=== "Iodinated Contrast in Pregnancy"

    !!! info "ICM in Pregnancy"
        - Iodinated contrast crosses the placenta; neonatal thyroid function may be transiently suppressed
        - **Use only when clinically necessary**
        - If administered: check neonatal thyroid function in first week after delivery

=== "Iodinated Contrast During Breastfeeding"

    - <1% of dose enters breast milk; <1% of that is absorbed
    - **ACR guidance:** Continue breastfeeding after iodinated contrast without interruption

## Contrast Extravasation

!!! warning "Extravasation Management"

    | Severity | Volume | Presentation | Management |
    |----------|--------|-------------|------------|
    | **Minor** | <10 mL of iso-osmolar or <2 mL of high-osmolar | Minimal pain/swelling | Elevate extremity, ice packs 15–20 min several times/day; monitor |
    | **Significant** | >10 mL of iso-osmolar or >2 mL of high-osmolar; hypo-osmolar agents; any volume with tissue ischemia symptoms | Marked swelling, blistering, vascular compromise | Elevate extremity, plastic surgery / wound care consultation; imaging if vascular compromise suspected |

    **Follow-up contact:** Advise patients to return if symptoms worsen, paresthesia, or skin changes develop.

## Thyroid and Iodinated Contrast

!!! warning "Iodinated Contrast and Thyroid Disease"

    - Large doses of iodinated contrast can cause **contrast-induced thyrotoxicosis** in susceptible patients (autonomous nodules, Graves' disease)
    - Patients with known hyperthyroidism or autonomous nodules: discuss with ordering physician and endocrinology prior to administering high-dose iodinated contrast
    - Routine patients without known thyroid disease: no special precautions needed
    - Contrast may interfere with **radioiodine therapy** — delay radioactive iodine treatment by 2–6 months after large contrast doses

## Contrast Reaction Risk Factors

| Factor | Increased Risk (ICM) | Increased Risk (GBCA) |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Prior reaction (same class) | Yes — major risk factor | Yes |
| Asthma | 2–3× increased risk | Slightly increased |
| Multiple allergies/atopy | Yes | Yes |
| Cardiac disease | For severe reactions | No |
| Renal impairment | CI-AKI risk | NSF risk |
| Anxiety | Yes (idiosyncratic) | No |
| Beta-blocker use | May worsen reaction, impairs epinephrine response | No |

## Common Pitfalls

!!! warning "Avoid These Mistakes"

    **❌ Don't:**

    - Treat contrast reactions with antihistamines alone — epinephrine is first-line for anaphylaxis
    - Use Group I/II GBCA agents in patients with eGFR <30 when alternatives exist
    - Delay epinephrine waiting for IV access — give IM first
    - Routinely hold metformin for eGFR ≥30 (outdated practice)

    **✅ Do:**

    - Have emergency medications and equipment immediately available in all contrast-administering areas
    - Check eGFR and relevant medications before contrast
    - Document contrast type, dose, lot number, and any reactions in the medical record
    - Choose macrocyclic GBCA when renal function is compromised
    - Brief all imaging staff on acute reaction protocols

## Emergency Equipment Checklist

??? tip "Required Emergency Supplies (Contrast Area)"

    - Epinephrine 1:1,000 (1 mg/mL) for IM injection
    - Oxygen supply and face masks
    - IV access supplies
    - Normal saline (1 L bags)
    - Diphenhydramine (IV/IM)
    - Atropine
    - Albuterol inhaler
    - Blood pressure cuff and pulse oximeter
    - Crash cart accessible within minutes
